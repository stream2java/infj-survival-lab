The Customs Office

July 2026

There's a customs office inside me.

Every time I want to say something, want something, or want to get close to someone — it has to clear customs first.

It took me a long time to notice. Not because it was hidden, but because it ran so smoothly I thought it was just who I was.

The Officer

There's an officer at the exit gate.

His job is to inspect all outgoing cargo: things I want to say, emotions I want to express, wants I have toward someone. He's diligent. He almost never takes a break.

He has a clearance policy:

  • Complaints: approved
  • Observations: approved
  • Analyses of other people's behavior: approved
  • "I want": detained

It's not that he refuses. It's that there are rules. "Want-category items" require a completed understanding procedure before they can be declared — confirm the other person can receive it, confirm the timing is right, confirm the wording won't cause a misunderstanding.

The problem is, the standard for "understanding complete" is set by him. And he can always ask for one more report.

So a lot of things expire in the waiting.

The Intelligence Department

Behind the officer, there's an office with no windows.

That's where the intelligence analysts work. Their job: predict how the other side will respond if this cargo goes through.

Their reports are very good. Too good — good enough that a lot of cargo gets cancelled by the sender before it even reaches customs.

"Intelligence says they probably won't accept it."

I've said that too many times. To friends, to people I was close to, to myself. But "probably" was never data. It was a very old risk assessment.

The Blacklist

Inside the intelligence database, there's a blacklist.

A long time ago, a shipment went through — and came back. With damage. The incident report was filed, and every "similar item" since then triggers an automatic alert.

The blacklist isn't wrong. That incident was real.

The problem is the weights were never updated. The system learned that expressing wants directly is high risk. It didn't learn that this was one specific outcome, in one specific situation. Not a universal law.

So now, every time an "I want" appears, the database assumes it will be rejected — no evidence required. Just similarity.

Imports Also Need Clearance

This is the part I figured out last.

Customs doesn't only manage exports. It manages imports too.

Warmth from others, positive signals, "you matter to me" — these incoming things go through customs as well. Held in a bonded warehouse. "Pending verification."

The database weights imports like this:

  • Negative signals: cleared immediately, delivered straight to the heart
  • Positive signals: held for observation, requires multiple supporting documents before release

Some of them sit there until they expire.

I used to think I couldn't receive care because I wasn't open enough. Now I know: it's not that I couldn't receive it. Nobody in the warehouse ever went to claim it.

Emergency Shutdown

There's one more thing.

Sometimes a shipment goes wrong — or looks like it's about to. The entire port shuts down. All flights grounded.

This isn't a decision. It's an explosion.

Pressure builds to a point, and then — it's not that I close the door. The door gets blown off. And the debris blocks every entrance.

I used to call it "disappearing." Stopped replying. Pulled away. Said "I'm fine" and went quiet. I thought I was choosing it. I wasn't. It was detonation.
